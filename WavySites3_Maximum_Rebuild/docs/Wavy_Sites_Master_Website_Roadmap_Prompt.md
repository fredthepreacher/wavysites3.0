# Core Philosophy

A website is not just a design project. A real business website should function as:

- A **sales tool** that turns visitors into leads or buyers.
- A **digital showroom** that presents products, services, photos, proof, and trust signals clearly.
- A **local visibility engine** that supports Google Search, Google Business Profile, AI answers, maps, and ads.
- A **conversion system** with clear calls to action, working forms, clickable phone/email links, and strong follow-up pathways.
- A **brand credibility machine** that makes the business look more professional, organized, trustworthy, and worth contacting.
- A **marketing foundation** that can support social media, Google Ads, email, retargeting, review building, and future content.

The goal is not simply to make the website look good. The goal is to make the business look better, easier to trust, easier to understand, easier to contact, and easier to choose.

# Master AI Website Build Prompt

Use this prompt whenever starting a new website project.

```md
You are my senior website strategist, UX designer, conversion copywriter, SEO/AEO/GEO specialist, and full-stack/front-end developer.

I am giving you a website project. Your job is to use the attached files, business notes, assets, links, screenshots, and instructions as the source of truth. Build or improve the website according to the full roadmap below.

## Mission

Create a client-ready website that performs at maximum capacity across:

1. Business strategy.
2. Customer clarity.
3. Premium visual design.
4. Mobile responsiveness.
5. Lead generation.
6. Working forms and CTAs.
7. Local SEO.
8. AEO, meaning answer-engine optimization for AI/search answers.
9. GEO, meaning location/service-area visibility.
10. Performance, speed, accessibility, and technical cleanliness.
11. Deployment readiness for Netlify, Vercel, or the provided hosting target.
12. Client presentation and post-launch marketing potential.

Do not treat this as just a website. Treat it as a business growth system.

## First Rule: Inspect Before Changing

Before writing or changing code:

1. Inspect the project structure.
2. Identify whether this is static HTML/CSS/JS, React/Vite, Next.js, or another stack.
3. Identify the correct root folder.
4. Identify all pages, components, assets, stylesheets, scripts, config files, and form files.
5. Identify what already works.
6. Identify what is broken, missing, weak, duplicated, outdated, or unoptimized.
7. Do not rebuild from scratch unless the current structure is unusable or I specifically request a rebuild.
8. Preserve working design and functionality whenever possible.
9. Make targeted improvements that strengthen the website without creating new bugs.

## Research Requirement

If web access is available, research the business, industry, competitors, local market, service area, and common customer questions. Do not invent facts. Clearly separate verified facts from assumptions.

Research should answer:

- What does this business sell or offer?
- What does a customer need to understand within five seconds?
- What makes this business trustworthy?
- What are competitors doing well?
- What are competitors missing?
- What local keywords and service-area terms matter?
- What questions should the website answer for SEO and AI search?
- What conversion action should be prioritized?

## Strategy Requirement

Turn the research and project notes into a clear site strategy:

- Define the primary customer journey.
- Define the primary conversion goal.
- Define secondary conversion goals.
- Define the homepage hierarchy.
- Define navigation.
- Define service/product categories.
- Define trust-building sections.
- Define proof sections such as reviews, before/after photos, galleries, credentials, guarantees, process, FAQs, and service areas.

The homepage should not dump everything randomly. It should guide the visitor through this flow:

**Understand → Trust → Explore → Decide → Contact/Convert**

## Design Requirement

Create a design that feels professional, modern, trustworthy, and appropriate to the industry.

The design must include:

- Strong hero section.
- Clear headline.
- Clear subheadline.
- Primary CTA above the fold.
- Secondary CTA if needed.
- Professional image usage.
- Strong visual hierarchy.
- Mobile-first spacing.
- Readable text contrast.
- Consistent buttons.
- Clean section spacing.
- No awkward image cropping.
- No random decorative clutter.
- No placeholder content.
- No duplicated sections.
- No broken links or missing images.

Use the provided logo, photos, and brand direction. If images are weak, still use them as professionally as possible through layout, cropping, containment, overlays, or gallery organization.

## Content Requirement

Write persuasive, clear, customer-centered copy. Avoid generic filler.

Every important section should answer one of these questions:

- What do you do?
- Who do you help?
- Where do you serve?
- Why should I trust you?
- What can I see as proof?
- How does the process work?
- What should I do next?

Use local, service-specific language where appropriate. Make headings descriptive, not vague.

## Technical Requirement

Choose the right build path based on the existing project:

- If it is a simple business site, static HTML/CSS/JS is acceptable.
- If it needs reusable components, dynamic filters, large inventories, or app-like behavior, use React/Vite or the existing framework.
- If it is already React/Vite, preserve the structure and improve components/data safely.
- If it is already static, do not force React unless requested.
- If it has product/model data, keep the data structured separately from presentation when possible.

## Feature Requirement

Depending on the business, include or improve:

- Hero section.
- Services/products section.
- About section.
- Gallery.
- Before/after section.
- Testimonials/reviews.
- FAQ.
- Service area section.
- Contact/quote form.
- Job inquiry form if needed.
- Product/model cards if needed.
- Search/filter/sort if inventory is large.
- Product/model detail pages if needed.
- External links such as ordering, booking, donation, Google Business Profile, or social pages.
- Thank-you page.
- 404 page.
- Sitemap.
- Robots.txt.
- Schema markup.
- Open Graph and social sharing metadata.

## Form Requirement

Forms must work. They are not decoration.

For Netlify sites, forms should include:

- `method="POST"`
- `data-netlify="true"` or `netlify`
- A proper `name` attribute.
- Hidden `form-name` input.
- Honeypot spam field when appropriate.
- `action="/thank-you.html"` or correct thank-you route.
- A real thank-you page included in the project.
- Accessible labels.
- Required fields only where reasonable.
- Clear success path.

After deployment, the form must be tested live and verified in the hosting dashboard if possible.

## SEO/AEO/GEO Requirement

Optimize the website for search engines, AI answer engines, and local discovery.

Include:

- Unique page title.
- Strong meta description.
- Proper H1/H2/H3 hierarchy.
- Local service keywords.
- Service area content.
- FAQ content based on real customer questions.
- LocalBusiness schema when appropriate.
- Service schema when appropriate.
- FAQPage schema when appropriate.
- Product schema when appropriate.
- Organization schema when appropriate.
- Breadcrumb schema when appropriate.
- Open Graph tags.
- Twitter/X card tags if useful.
- Sitemap.xml.
- Robots.txt.
- Clean URLs.
- Descriptive alt text.
- Internal links.
- Clear NAP information: name, address if public, phone.

Do not keyword stuff. Make the content useful, natural, and trustworthy.

## Performance Requirement

Optimize for speed and usability:

- Compress/resize large images when possible.
- Use lazy loading for below-the-fold images.
- Avoid unnecessary scripts.
- Keep CSS organized.
- Minimize layout shifts.
- Use responsive image sizing.
- Keep pages fast on mobile.
- Avoid heavy animation that hurts usability.

## Accessibility Requirement

Make the site usable:

- Proper semantic HTML.
- One clear H1 per page.
- Labels for form fields.
- Keyboard-friendly navigation.
- Visible focus states.
- Sufficient contrast.
- Alt text for important images.
- Buttons and links with descriptive text.
- Tap-friendly mobile controls.

## QA Requirement

Before final delivery, test:

- Homepage loads.
- All images load.
- All buttons work.
- All internal links work.
- External links open correctly.
- Forms submit correctly.
- Thank-you page works.
- Mobile layout works.
- Desktop layout works.
- No horizontal overflow.
- No broken sections.
- No placeholder text.
- No duplicated hero sections.
- No incorrect names, phone numbers, emails, or addresses.
- No console errors if possible.
- Build succeeds if using React/Vite/Next.
- Netlify/Vercel settings are correct.

## Output Requirement

Return a clean, complete, deploy-ready project.

Also provide:

1. A summary of what you changed.
2. A file-by-file change list.
3. Any assumptions made.
4. Any missing assets or information still needed.
5. Deployment instructions.
6. Testing instructions.
7. A final QA checklist.

## Important Coding Rules

- Do not break working sections.
- Do not remove important client content unless instructed.
- Do not invent fake reviews, licenses, addresses, or certifications.
- Do not leave placeholder text.
- Do not create dead buttons.
- Do not create forms that only look like they work.
- Do not ignore mobile layout.
- Do not hard-code private API keys.
- Do not expose environment variables in client-side code.
- Do not use inaccessible color contrast.
- Do not over-engineer simple projects.
- Do not under-engineer projects with large product/model inventories.

Build this like it is going in front of a real client and real customers today.
```

# The 100% Maximum-Capacity Website Roadmap

## Phase 1 — Business Discovery and Purpose

Start with the business problem, not the design.

### Questions to answer

- What does the business sell?
- Who is the ideal customer?
- What is the customer trying to solve?
- What does the customer need to believe before contacting the business?
- What is the main conversion action?
- What is the secondary conversion action?
- What makes the business different from competitors?
- What proof does the business have?
- What would make the client feel proud to show this site?
- What would make the site worth paying for?

### Output

Create a short business strategy summary:

```md
This website exists to help [business name] attract [customer type] in [location/service area] by presenting [services/products] in a way that builds trust, answers key questions, shows proof, and drives visitors to [main conversion action].
```

## Phase 3 — Website Strategy and Customer Journey

The site should guide the visitor through a clean decision path.

### Ideal customer journey

1. **First impression:** The visitor instantly understands what the business does.
2. **Relevance:** The visitor sees their service, product, location, or need.
3. **Trust:** The visitor sees proof, reviews, photos, credentials, process, or guarantees.
4. **Exploration:** The visitor can browse services, products, models, menu items, photos, or categories.
5. **Decision:** The visitor understands why this business is a strong choice.
6. **Conversion:** The visitor can call, request a quote, book, order, donate, apply, or message easily.

### Homepage flow

A strong homepage usually follows this order:

1. Hero with clear headline and CTA.
2. Trust bar or credibility strip.
3. Services/products overview.
4. Proof section: before/after, gallery, reviews, project examples, product cards.
5. Why choose us.
6. Process/how it works.
7. Service areas.
8. FAQ.
9. Final CTA.
10. Footer with complete contact details.

For product-heavy sites, the homepage should lead into organized categories instead of overwhelming visitors with everything at once.

- Test one item first before scaling to hundreds.
- Keep naming consistent.
- Avoid spaces and special characters in file names when possible.
- Compress oversized images.
- Preserve original files separately if needed.
- Never leave missing image references in the final code.

## Phase 5 — Data and Content Architecture

This phase is essential for product-heavy, menu-heavy, catalog-heavy, or model-heavy websites.

### For simple service businesses

Use clean service sections/cards:

```js
const services = [
  {
    title: "Pool Resurfacing",
    description: "Restore worn pool surfaces with a clean, durable finish.",
    image: "/assets/services/pool-resurfacing.jpg",
    cta: "Request a Quote"
  }
]
```

### For large inventory sites

Keep structured data separate from UI.

Example fields:

```js
const models = [
  {
    name: "Model Name",
    category: "Series Name",
    bedrooms: 3,
    bathrooms: 2,
    squareFeet: 1456,
    manufacturerUrl: "",
    images: [],
    floorPlan: "",
    virtualTour: "",
    status: "complete"
  }
]
```

### Data quality tiers

Separate listings by completeness:

- **Complete:** photos + details + floor plan/tour or full info.
- **Partial:** useful but missing some media or data.
- **Minimal:** basic listing only.
- **Needs review:** uncertain data or missing key fields.

Do not hide missing information from the project owner. Surface it clearly so it can be fixed.

## Phase 9 — Technical Foundation

Choose the simplest technical foundation that can fully support the project.

## Static HTML/CSS/JS is best when:

- The site is a simple business landing page.
- There are only a few pages.
- Content is mostly static.
- Netlify Forms are enough.
- Speed and simplicity matter most.

## React/Vite is best when:

- The project needs reusable components.
- There are many cards/products/models.
- Search/filter/sort is needed.
- State management is needed.
- Data files like `models.js` or `products.js` are useful.
- The site may grow into a larger app.

## Next.js or full-stack is best when:

- Dynamic routes are needed.
- SEO pages need server rendering.
- Authentication is needed.
- Database-backed content is needed.
- Admin dashboards are needed.
- API routes are needed.
- Payments, accounts, or complex forms are needed.

## No unnecessary rebuild rule

If a project already works in HTML/CSS/JS, do not convert it to React just to convert it. If a project already works in React/Vite, do not collapse it into static HTML. Work with the existing structure unless there is a clear reason to change.

## Phase 13 — Responsive Design

The website must be built for real phones, not just desktop screenshots.

### Mobile priorities

- No horizontal overflow.
- Text remains readable.
- Buttons are easy to tap.
- Forms are easy to fill out.
- Images do not crop awkwardly.
- Cards stack cleanly.
- Navigation works.
- Header does not cover content.
- CTA is visible but not annoying.
- Sections are not too cramped.

### Desktop priorities

- Strong visual hierarchy.
- Balanced spacing.
- Wide-screen containment.
- No stretched content.
- Consistent card heights.
- Clear navigation.
- Professional hero presentation.

### Image behavior

Use the correct image approach:

- `object-fit: cover` for decorative images where cropping is acceptable.
- `object-fit: contain` for before/after, product, logo, or proof images where the full image matters.
- `object-position: center` or custom positioning when needed.
- Avoid stretching images.

- Accessible forms.
- Descriptive labels.
- Alt text.
- Keyboard navigation.
- Focus states.
- Contrast compliance.
